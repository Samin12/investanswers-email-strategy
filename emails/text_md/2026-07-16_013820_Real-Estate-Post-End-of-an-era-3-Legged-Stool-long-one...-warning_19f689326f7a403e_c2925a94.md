 Hey everybody, just wanted to share why IвҖҷve been a little quiet the last day or so.

 For decades I believed in the classic three-legged stool вҖ” and real estate was always one of the main legs. Made money in markets, too that profit and bought multi unit apt buildings, renovated them, made them cash flow and then plan was to hold forever. So if I lost everything in the markets I always had cashflow engines. That leg is gone now (aside from the personal real estate I keep for my own family use).

 The situation in San Francisco had become untenable. Rent control, combined with social activism, shifting legal risks, and steadily rising costs, made the economics stop making sense. Not to mention the clear and present danger of liability. 

 HereвҖҷs a clear example: one unit had a tenant whoвҖҷd been there 42 years. We were collecting $900 a month while the unitвҖҷs market reality was closer to $5,000+. Every month it was costing me roughly $4,100 net a month for decades вҖ” pure dead money.

CAGR = 2.8114% which is 12% under what I need to tread water. Rem in SF If those had been single-family homes instead, they likely would have sold for 3вҖ“4 times as much, even with no rental income.

 Final note, SF is red hot due to the AI Boom... so I consider it a top of the market and next year RE could probably tank so I was happy to exit before the summer ended. 

 Between that math, higher property taxes and utilities, outrageous insurance for $10K to $20K per building, and increasing liability, it just didnвҖҷt make sense to keep holding. Better to sell, take the capital, and put it somewhere with real upside.

 If this were almost anywhere else in the U.S., the decision might have been different. But rent control in San Francisco proved to be the kiss of death for landlords trying to run viable real estate business. Same thing in NYC, my friends who had multi properties there they are selling them for a fraction of what they were worth a few years ago just to escape New York.
