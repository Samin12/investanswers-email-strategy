 Alex Potter is a Managing Director and senior research analyst at Piper Sandler who covers Tesla and related sectors. Like most sell-side equity analysts, he operates under strict firm and regulatory compliance policies that generally prohibit analysts from owning shares in the companies they cover. The intent is to protect research objectivity and avoid conflicts of interest.

 In a recent CyberBulls interview, Potter offered this candid personal view:

 “So, me personally, like if I was allowed to own stocks in my own portfolio that I cover, which I’m not, but if I was allowed, um, then Tesla would be the one that I hold and I would just hold it until the day I retire.”

 What stands out is the clear tension here. Potter articulates exceptionally high long-term conviction in Tesla — essentially saying it would be his primary holding for the rest of his career if permitted — yet standard industry rules prevent him from investing in it at all.

 To me this creates a somewhat paradoxical situation: the professionals with the deepest access, modeling, and insight into a company’s multi-decade potential are often the ones most restricted from acting on that view with their own capital. Meanwhile, individual investors face no such barriers.
