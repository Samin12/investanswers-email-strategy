 Anthropic update On the competitor side, Anthropic has confidentially filed to go public. They’re aiming to IPO sometime between September and November 2026 (depending on how fast regulators move). This means Anthropic could become a public company before OpenAI does.

 OpenAI’s CFO just explained how the company sees itself differently from competitors like Anthropic, especially when it comes to who’s actually winning with the smartest AI models.

 She pointed out that ChatGPT has become a household name — like when people say “Google it” when they mean search. It’s now both a product and a verb. The company is also seeing surprisingly strong growth in places most people wouldn’t expect, like Africa and Kazakhstan.

 OpenAI basically runs two businesses at once:

 A consumer business — regular people chatting with ChatGPT every day

 An enterprise business — companies paying for more powerful versions

 Here’s how much people actually use it:

 Free users ask about 7 questions per day

 People paying $20/month ask about 20 questions per day

 Pro users ask about 70 questions per day

 The biggest problem right now: Power and computers The biggest thing holding OpenAI back isn’t ideas or talent — it’s electricity and computing power.

 Building enough power infrastructure is extremely expensive. For example, one gigawatt of power (which costs around $50 billion to set up) might eventually bring in only about $10 billion in revenue. The numbers don’t really add up. That’s why OpenAI is working with other companies (like Oracle and CoreWeave) to rent computing power instead of buying everything themselves. This lets them spread out the huge upfront costs.

 Nvidia is still their main chip supplier, but they’re also working with AMD and Broadcom, and they’re even developing their own chips. They’re also building a big new data center in Texas with SoftBank.

 Looking ahead In the future, things will get more complicated. Cloud companies (like Google) will have their own advanced AI models, and chip makers like Nvidia will also be building AI models themselves.

 OpenAI does plan to add advertising eventually. Right now, they make the most money by selling access to their technology through their API. In the future, they want to reach everyday people at a much larger scale.

 Current shortages Right now, OpenAI is limited by several major bottlenecks:

 Not enough electricity

 Not enough server racks

 Not enough chips

 Not enough memory

 Not enough specialized talent

 And even trust — making sure AI doesn’t leave regular people or communities behind

 The CFO was pretty blunt: It’s already hard to buy the computing power you need in 2026, and 2027 will still be very limited. When someone mentioned that Elon Musk has access to compute, she didn’t really respond. They even had to cancel (or pause) their video generation tool Sora because they simply didn’t have enough computing power to run it.
